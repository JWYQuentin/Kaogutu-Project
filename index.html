Part I — Examine

What Is the Kaogu Tu?

The Kaogu Tu (考古圖), or "Illustrated Catalogue of Examined Antiquity," is a ten-volume compendium of ancient Chinese artifacts compiled during the Northern Song dynasty. Completed in 1092 CE, it is recognized as the oldest surviving systematic illustrated catalog of ancient objects — a work that predates European antiquarian cataloging by centuries.

The catalog documents 210 bronze vessels and 13 jade objects dating from the Shang dynasty (c. 1600–1046 BCE) through the Han dynasty (206 BCE – 220 CE). Each entry includes a detailed illustration drawn to scale, measurements and dimensions, notes on provenance (whether the item was held in a private collection or by an imperial institution), and transcriptions of any inscriptions found on the object.

Far from a simple collector's list, the Kaogu Tu represents a deliberate effort to study the past through material evidence rather than textual tradition alone — an approach that foreshadows modern archaeological method.

Full Title

考古圖 (Kaogu Tu) — "Illustrated Catalogue of Examined Antiquity," with a supplement titled 續考古圖 (Xu Kaogu Tu) in 5 additional volumes.

Date of Completion

1092 CE (7th year of the Yuanyou era, Northern Song dynasty), with a preface bearing this date.

Format & Binding

Traditional Chinese woodblock print. The earliest surviving print dates to the Song period. Later Ming and Qing editions are more widely circulated.

Illustrations

Functional, not decorative. Each drawing records the shape, proportions, and inscriptions of ancient bronze and jade objects as empirical evidence for study.

Supplement

The Xu Kaogu Tu (續考古圖) adds 5 volumes covering jade objects, additional bronzes, bells, and other ritual instruments.

Accompanying Text

The Kaogu Tu Shiwen (考古圖釋文) provides character-by-character explanations of inscriptions and ancient scripts found on the cataloged objects.

Part II — Research

The Scholar Behind the Catalog

呂大臨
Lü Dalin
c. 1044 – 1093 CE
Philosopher · Epigrapher · Collector
National University Erudite (博士)
Northern Song Dynasty

Lü Dalin was a Northern Song scholar who moved between the worlds of Neo-Confucian philosophy and material antiquarianism. A disciple of the renowned philosopher Zhang Zai and the Cheng brothers (Cheng Hao and Cheng Yi), Lü received rigorous training in classical learning before turning his attention to physical artifacts.

He held the position of boshi (博士, erudite) at the imperial National University (太學), and was an avid collector of books and cultural objects. His scholarly life combined philosophical inquiry with a commitment to studying material evidence — an unusual combination for his era.

Lü died before completing a promotion to the Imperial Secretariat, but his Kaogu Tu survived as his defining legacy. In the preface, he criticizes both the ancient Daoist philosopher Zhuangzi (for dismissing the study of the past) and his own contemporaries (for their ignorance of ancient objects). This dual critique reveals a scholar driven by the conviction that physical artifacts could illuminate historical truth in ways that texts alone could not.

Inside the Book

A Systematic Catalog of Ancient Objects

Lü Dalin organized the ten volumes by vessel type — a classification system that reveals a systematic, almost scientific approach to cataloging antiquity. The objects span roughly 1,500 years of Chinese civilization.

I
Juan 1
18 tripod cauldrons (鼎 dǐng) — ritual vessels used in state ceremonies and ancestral rites.
II
Juan 2
12 hollow-foot tripods (鬲 lì), 6 tripod-and-pot sets (甗 yǎn), and 1 special tripod (鬵 zǎn).
III
Juan 3
16 round vessels (敦 duì), 4 tureens (簋 guǐ), pots (缶 fǒu), kettles (鋪 pū), and a tureen cover.
IV
Juan 4
21 ritual vessels (彝 yí), 8 carry-on pots (卣 yǒu), 3 wine vessels (尊 zūn), 1 jar (罍 léi), and 14 vases (壺 hú).
V–X
Juan 5–10
Additional bronze types including wine warmers, water vessels, musical instruments, weapons, and miscellaneous implements.
續
Supplement (5 Juan)
20 jade objects, 22 additional bronzes, 26 tripods, 20 bells, and 12 miscellaneous bronze artifacts.
Cataloging Method

How Lü Dalin Documented Antiquity

What makes the Kaogu Tu remarkable is not merely its scope, but its method. Each catalog entry follows a consistent, empirical framework that anticipates the rigor of modern archaeological documentation.

圖

Illustration

A detailed drawing of the object, rendered to show its form, proportions, and decorative patterns as accurately as possible.

量

Measurement

Precise dimensions including height, width, depth, and weight — a standardized approach to physical documentation.

銘

Inscription & Provenance

Transcription and analysis of inscriptions, plus records of where the object was held — imperial archives, temples, or private collections.

"Lü Dalin tried to avoid all speculation and wrong interpretation. The various objects had been collected by private collectors as well as by government institutions."
Historical Context

The Song Dynasty Golden Age of Antiquarianism

The Kaogu Tu did not emerge in isolation. It was part of a broader Northern Song intellectual movement that turned to ancient objects as sources of historical knowledge — a movement now recognized as the birth of Chinese antiquarianism.

1007–1072
Ouyang Xiu and the Jigulu (集古錄)
The great statesman and scholar Ouyang Xiu compiles a catalog of some 400 rubbings of ancient inscriptions on bronze and stone — pioneering the field of epigraphy (金石學, jīnshí xué) and establishing the precedent that ancient objects could serve as historical evidence.
c. 1044
Birth of Lü Dalin
Born into a scholarly family, Lü Dalin becomes a student of the philosopher Zhang Zai and the Cheng brothers, inheriting both Neo-Confucian thought and an empirical mindset.
1070s–1080s
Growth of Private Collections
Wealthy Song scholars and officials amass collections of ancient bronzes, jades, and stone inscriptions. Government institutions — the Imperial Archives, Palace Treasury, and Office of Sacrifices — also accumulate significant holdings, creating a critical mass of available objects for study.
1092
Completion of the Kaogu Tu
Lü Dalin finishes his systematic catalog of 210 bronzes and 13 jades from the Shang through Han dynasties, drawing from both private and imperial collections. The work sets a new standard for the documentation of ancient objects.
c. 1093
Death of Lü Dalin
Lü dies shortly after completing his masterwork, before receiving a promotion to the Imperial Secretariat. His catalog survives as his most enduring contribution.
1111–1125
Emperor Huizong's Bogu Tu (博古圖)
Emperor Huizong commissions a far larger catalog of the imperial collection — the Xuanhe Bogu Tu — containing 30 volumes. It builds directly on the model established by Lü Dalin's Kaogu Tu, confirming the latter's foundational influence.
1127
Fall of the Northern Song
The Jurchen conquest destroys Kaifeng and scatters Huizong's vast collection. The Kaogu Tu and Bogu Tu become some of the only surviving records of objects that were otherwise lost — elevating their importance as historical documents.
14th–19th c.
Transmission Through Later Dynasties
The Kaogu Tu is reprinted in the Ming and Qing periods, and incorporated into the Siku Quanshu (四庫全書), the largest collection of texts in Chinese history. It remains a standard reference for bronze studies across centuries.
20th c. – Present
Modern Recognition
Scholars recognize the Kaogu Tu as a precursor to modern archaeology. Its systematic approach to classification, measurement, and provenance recording anticipates methods formalized in Western archaeology only in the 18th and 19th centuries. Digital editions now make the text globally accessible.
Archaeological Significance

Precursor to Modern Archaeology

The Kaogu Tu occupies a unique place in the global history of archaeology. While the word "考古" (kǎogǔ) — which literally means "examining antiquity" — would eventually become the modern Chinese term for "archaeology," the discipline as practiced by Lü Dalin was centuries ahead of comparable Western efforts.

In 11th-century Europe, the study of ancient objects was largely confined to the collecting of Roman coins and relics, with little systematic description. Lü Dalin's catalog, by contrast, applied a consistent methodology: each object was illustrated, measured, classified by type, and placed in a chronological framework spanning dynasties. Inscriptions were transcribed and interpreted. Provenance — whether the piece came from a private collection or an imperial institution — was recorded.

These are the core practices of modern archaeological documentation. The Kaogu Tu accomplished them nine centuries ago.

Enduring Legacy

Why the Kaogu Tu Still Matters

Nearly a millennium after its creation, the Kaogu Tu remains relevant to multiple fields of study. Its influence extends far beyond its original Song dynasty context.

Foundation of Bronze Studies

The typological classification system Lü Dalin developed for ancient bronzes — grouping objects by form (鼎, 鬲, 簋, etc.) — remains the foundation of Chinese bronze vessel studies today. Modern catalogs still use categories he helped establish.

Record of Lost Objects

Many artifacts documented in the Kaogu Tu were subsequently lost — particularly after the fall of the Northern Song in 1127. The catalog serves as the only surviving record of objects that would otherwise be unknown to history.

Epigraphic Resource

The inscription transcriptions in the Kaogu Tu Shiwen (釋文) remain valuable for scholars studying ancient Chinese scripts, providing readings of bronze inscriptions that can be cross-referenced with modern archaeological finds.

Model for Later Catalogs

Emperor Huizong's Bogu Tu, the Qing dynasty's epigraphic tradition, and ultimately modern Chinese archaeology all trace a lineage back to the systematic methods pioneered in Lü Dalin's work.
